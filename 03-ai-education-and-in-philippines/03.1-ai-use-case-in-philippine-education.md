# Artificial Intelligence in Philippine Education: A Narrative Review
**Authors:** Stephen Jay Co (Far Eastern University)
**Published:** International Research Journal of Science, Technology, Education, and Management, Volume 5, No. 2 | June 2025

---

## Overview

This narrative review analyzes the current status of AI implementation in Philippine educational settings through a comprehensive synthesis of **43 scholarly publications from 2018 to 2025**. The study used Braun and Clarke's thematic analytical approach to examine AI applications, stakeholder perceptions, and implementation challenges across different educational levels.

---

## Background and Context

### The Global Landscape
AI technologies are revolutionizing education worldwide through:
- Personalized learning platforms
- Automated assessment tools
- Intelligent tutoring systems
- Streamlined administrative operations

Contemporary research has established AI's capabilities to improve academic achievement through individualized learning trajectories, minimize administrative workload, and enable educational data analysis.

### The Philippine Context
The Philippines presents a valuable case study because it embodies the tension between **technological aspiration and implementation realities**—a developing nation with a strong tradition of educational innovation yet persistent infrastructure challenges.

Key contextual factors:
- The COVID-19 pandemic accelerated technology adoption through the Basic Education-Learning Continuity Plan (BE-LCP)
- CHED's flexible learning directives pushed technological modernization
- Research on AI adoption in Philippine education remains fragmented
- The country's unique socioeconomic characteristics require contextual understanding rather than adopting approaches from high-resource settings

---

## Research Objectives

The study aimed to answer: *What are the main themes in AI applications and adoption in Philippine education from 2018 to 2025, and what contextual factors influence these themes?*

Specifically, the review sought to:
1. Determine present AI implementations across different educational levels
2. Examine attitudes and preparedness of educators, learners, and administrators
3. Identify challenges and issues in implementing AI in Philippine educational contexts

## Methodology

### Search Strategy
- **Databases searched:** Google Scholar, EBSCO Host, ScienceDirect, and specialized repositories
- **Search terms:** "Artificial Intelligence," "AI," "ChatGPT," "Generative AI," "Philippines," "Education," "Teacher"
- **Timeframe:** January 2018 to March 2025

### Selection Process (PRISMA)
| Stage | Count |
|-------|-------|
| Records identified through database searching | 1,247 |

| EBSCO Host | 234 |
| ScienceDirect | 121 |

- 2020-2022: 13 studies (30.2%)
- 2023-2025: 30 studies (69.8%)

## Theme 1: Current AI Applications in Philippine Education

The review identified four main application areas, with implementation concentrated in higher education institutions, particularly urban private universities.

### 1.1 Teaching and Learning Tools

**Higher Education:**
- Multiple universities have deployed AI-enhanced learning management systems offering personalized learning pathways and adaptive content delivery
- Espartinez (2024) documented generative AI use for composition teaching across eight institutions—primarily instructor-led rather than institutionally mandated
- Barrot (2024) explored ChatGPT integration for second language writing, finding implementation was largely experimental and confined to specific courses

**K-12 Level:**
- AI applications are more limited
- Prestoza and Banatao (2024) reported on a pilot implementation of AI-driven personalized learning in science education at select urban secondary schools
- Found measurable improvements in student engagement but significant challenges in scaling beyond pilot phase
- Elementary schools showed minimal AI implementation beyond basic educational applications with rudimentary AI features (Fontanilla et al., 2023)

**Intelligent Tutoring Systems:**
- Hatmanto et al. (2024) found that senior teachers in Mindanao effectively utilized AI to create more personalized learning experiences despite initial skepticism

### 1.2 Student Support Systems

**Higher Education:**
- Agbong-Coates (2024) studied 785 college students and found that integrated AI systems significantly enhanced personalized learning outcomes
- Support systems range from AI chatbots for common queries to sophisticated systems for identifying at-risk students

**K-12 Level:**
- Fabro et al. (2024) found limited implementation of sophisticated student support AI
- Existing technologies primarily focused on basic administrative functions
- Exception: Private international schools in Metro Manila have implemented AI-driven learning analytics

### 1.3 Administrative Applications

More uniformly distributed across educational levels:
- Luciano (2024) documented AI use for automating routine tasks: scheduling, document processing, preliminary admission assessments
- Ramos et al. (2024) examined ChatGPT in secondary school administration, finding efficiency improvements but challenges in data privacy and security

### 1.4 Research Tools

Primarily in tertiary education:
- Bula et al. (2025) found widespread adoption of tools like ChatGPT for literature reviews and initial data analysis among library science students
- Particularly pronounced in STEM disciplines where machine learning is increasingly incorporated into research methodologies

## Theme 2: Stakeholder Perceptions and Readiness

### 2.1 Teachers and Faculty Perceptions

**Mixed attitudes toward AI integration:**

| Finding | Source |
|---------|--------|
| Strong confidence despite minimal structured preparation | Sumandal (2024) - 25 educators at Abueg National High School |
| Moderate awareness and generally positive attitudes, with concerns about overreliance | Goli-Cruz (2024) |
| Significant reservations about academic integrity and replacement of instructional roles | Candilas et al. (2025) |
| Concerns more pronounced among older faculty and those with limited tech exposure | Suello et al. (2024) |

**Readiness Assessment:**
- Bautista et al. (2024) assessed pre-service teachers using the TPACK framework
- Finding: Technical knowledge is developing, but **pedagogical integration remains a significant challenge**
- This theme of technical capability outpacing pedagogical integration was consistent across multiple studies

### 2.2 Student Perceptions

Generally more positive than faculty:

- **Patac and Patac (2025):** Students using ChatGPT for academic support reported reduced cognitive load and enhanced learning efficiency
- **Obenza et al. (2024):** University students showed strong comprehension, awareness, and favorable perspectives toward AI in tertiary education
- **Suarez et al. (2025):** Secondary students expressed greater enthusiasm for AI in STEM subjects compared to humanities, where concerns about creativity and critical thinking were more prominent
- **Villarosa (2024):** College students' awareness varied significantly by program—technology-focused majors showed greater awareness and positive perceptions

**Usage and Achievement:**
- Navales (2024): Teachers' perceptions of ChatGPT's usefulness strongly correlated with willingness to incorporate it
- Bancoro (2024): Moderate AI integration yielded positive outcomes, but overreliance raised concerns about skill development

### 2.3 Administrator and Policymaker Perspectives

Research in this area was more limited:
- **Piedad et al. (2025):** Found inconsistent regulatory frameworks and significant variations in institutional readiness across Philippine universities
- **Giray et al. (2024):** Administrators and instructors collectively acknowledged AI as transformative with potential benefits alongside ethical concerns

**Critical Gap Identified:**
A disconnect between institutional policy and practical implementation—educational institutions struggle to develop comprehensive policies that address both technological innovations and traditional academic values (Plata et al., 2023).

## Theme 3: Implementation Challenges

### 3.1 Infrastructure and Resource Limitations

**Connectivity and Hardware:**
- Melchor et al. (2023): Significant infrastructure limitations in mathematics education, especially in remote regions with restricted network access
- Villarino (2025): Rural higher education institutions face substantial challenges due to connectivity issues and outdated hardware

**Funding Constraints:**
- Digo et al. (2024): High costs of AI system acquisition, deployment, and maintenance present significant challenges, particularly for public institutions
- Financial constraints often result in piecemeal implementation rather than systematic integration

**Urban-Rural Digital Divide:**
- Balasa et al. (2025): Significant variations in AI implementation between schools in Zamboanga Peninsula—urban schools demonstrate greater adoption rates
- This divide was consistent across multiple studies (Diaz, 2025; Bula et al., 2025)

### 3.2 Knowledge and Skills Gaps

**Technical Expertise:**
- Alejandro et al. (2024): Pre-service teachers' AI adoption was restricted by perceived difficulty of use and lack of training
- Barrot (2023): Knowledge gaps among educators identified as a key challenge

**Professional Development:**
- De Jesus (2024): Teachers who received specific training on ChatGPT demonstrated significantly greater integration and effectiveness
- Fontanilla et al. (2023): Restricted capacity-building opportunities identified as a key constraint

**Student Digital Literacy:**
- Rom and Soriano (2025): Varying levels of AI literacy among students with implications for equitable access
- Asio (2024): Substantial differences in AI knowledge, confidence, and capability among university learners depending on college, year level, and gender

### 3.3 Ethical and Sociocultural Considerations

**Academic Integrity:**
- Gustilo et al. (2023): Significant ethical concerns around academic integrity and potential plagiarism in AI-assisted academic writing
- Barrot (2024): Highlighted tensions between technological assistance and academic honesty

**Data Privacy:**
- Obenza et al. (2024): Students expressed concerns about data privacy and ethical implications despite positive perceptions
- Giray et al. (2024): Administrators echoed these concerns

**Cultural Appropriateness:**
- Calizo and Bauyot (2025): Many AI systems were not designed with Filipino cultural contexts in mind, potentially limiting effectiveness
- Candilas (2025): Language barriers could impede effective use of AI tools primarily designed for English-language contexts

### 3.4 Policy and Governance Issues

**Lack of Comprehensive Policies:**
- Bongcac and Pabalan (2024): Clear absence of institutional regulations managing AI tool implementation
- Funa and Gabay (2025): Scattered character of current regulations among institutions

**Regulatory Uncertainty:**
- Piedad et al. (2025): Inconsistent approaches across institutions and lack of national guidance
- Created challenges for institutions seeking to implement AI while maintaining academic standards

**Institutional Readiness Variations:**
- Cacho (2024): Organizational preparedness significantly influenced implementation success
- De Guzman et al. (2024): Differing degrees of AI apprehension and preparedness among English educators and learners

## Discussion: Key Patterns

### Higher Education vs. Basic Education Gap

The implementation gap represents one of the most significant findings:
- Universities possess greater institutional flexibility and autonomy
- Competitive pressures and research mandates drive rapid decision-making
- K-12 schools face constraints from standardized curricula and centralized oversight

The concentration in STEM disciplines reflects:
- Disciplinary cultures embracing innovation
- Faculty technical expertise
- Research applications driving adoption

### Generational and Positional Divide

- Students ("digital natives") demonstrate greater comfort with emerging technologies
- Student enthusiasm creates bottom-up pressure for adoption that often outpaces formal policy development
- Faculty complexity: positive attitudes toward potential benefits alongside concerns about integrity and displacement
- Divergent stakeholder attitudes directly shape implementation success

### Developing Nation Challenges

The Philippines' position as a developing nation with infrastructure disparities creates:
- Equity concerns about AI worsening existing educational inequalities
- Patterns similar to other ASEAN developing nations (Indonesia, Vietnam)
- However, the Philippines' English language proficiency provides unique advantages for AI tool adoption

---

## Conclusions

The review reveals a landscape characterized by:
- Emerging implementation concentrated in urban higher education
- Mixed stakeholder perceptions with generational divides
- Significant contextual challenges requiring Philippine-specific solutions

**Effective AI implementation requires coordinated action across:**
- Technological readiness
- Policy frameworks
- Infrastructure capacity
- Human resource development

---

## Key Recommendations

### Three Urgent Priorities

1. **Develop a National AI Education Policy**
   - Address current regulatory gaps
   - Reduce uncertainty impeding systematic implementation
   - Provide guidance for institutions

2. **Invest in Rural Digital Infrastructure**
   - Address the persistent urban-rural divide
   - Prevent deepening of existing educational inequalities
   - Ensure equitable access to AI-enhanced learning

3. **Implement Comprehensive Professional Development**
   - Bridge critical knowledge gaps among educators
   - Move beyond technical training
   - Incorporate pedagogical frameworks for effective AI integration

### Stakeholder Actions

| Stakeholder | Recommended Action |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Government officials | Create nationwide structures guaranteeing fair access |
| Teachers | Participate actively in capacity building |
| Academic administrators | Advance from trial programs to holistic AI approaches |

## Future Research Directions

- Extended studies monitoring AI effects on educational achievements in underserved K-12 environments
- Comparative research examining implementation across different socioeconomic contexts
- Policy research on governance frameworks
- Development of culturally responsive evaluation frameworks specific to the Philippine context
- Cross-disciplinary collaboration incorporating education, technology, and policy sciences

---

## Study Limitations

1. Rapidly evolving nature of AI means the review cannot capture all relevant implementations
2. Focus on published research may underrepresent practical applications not yet studied
3. Disproportionate representation of higher education studies compared to basic education research
