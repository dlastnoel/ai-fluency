Module 1 › The Turing Test: Limitations
What the Turing Test Cannot Tell Us
Despite being a landmark idea, the test has significant limitations that are still debated today.
  • No measure of true understanding. The test only measures whether output resembles human conversation — not whether the machine actually comprehends what it is saying.
  • Subjective evaluation. The judgment depends entirely on the human judge's own understanding of how humans communicate — making results inconsistent.
  • The Confederate Effect. Sometimes a human interlocutor is mistakenly identified as a machine — exposing the test's reliance on expectation rather than truth.
  • Question selection matters. If questions favor uniquely human abilities (creativity, empathy), the machine is more likely to be exposed — making the test highly sensitive to design choices.
💡 The Turing Test remains one of AI's most famous ideas — but today, we recognize that passing the test does not equal true intelligence.
Source: Turing, A.M. (1950). Computing Machinery and Intelligence. Mind.
The Story of AI — Part 1
Birth of AI
1940s – 1950s
  • 1943 McCulloch & Pitts propose that the brain could be modeled as a computing system — introducing artificial neurons, the foundation of today's neural networks.
  • 1950 Alan Turing publishes "Computing Machinery and Intelligence" — asking "Can machines think?" and proposing the Turing Test.
  • 1956 John McCarthy organizes a workshop at Dartmouth College and coins the term "Artificial Intelligence." This event is considered the official birthday of the field.
Source: Coursera Staff (2025). How Does AI Work? Basics to Know.
The Story of AI — Part 2
Early Programs & AI Winters
1960s – 1990s
Early Promises ✅
  • ELIZA (1966) — The first chatbot. Fooled users into thinking it was a real therapist by mirroring their responses.
  • MYCIN — Expert system that helped diagnose blood infections using "if-then" rules.
  • Deep Blue (1997) — IBM's chess AI defeats world champion Garry Kasparov — the first machine to beat a reigning human champion.
The AI Winters ❄️
  • Progress could not keep up with the hype. Researchers over-promised and under-delivered.
  • A critical 1973 report (Lighthill Report) argued AI had failed its promises — leading to massive funding cuts. First AI Winter.
  • A second winter followed in the late 1980s as expert systems proved costly and fragile.
  • But the groundwork was still being laid: backpropagation (1986) gave neural networks a way to learn from mistakes.
Source: Coursera Staff (2025). How Does AI Work?

The Story of AI — Part 4
The Generative AI Era
2020 – Present
Generative AI refers to systems that create new content — text, images, audio, video, and code — by learning patterns from massive datasets. This is the era of ChatGPT, DALL-E, and Gemini.
What Makes This Era Different
  • GPT-3 (2020) — 175 billion parameters. AI could generate human-like text with minimal training.
  • ChatGPT (2022) — Made generative AI accessible to everyone. Reached 100 million users in 2 months.
  • 800 million weekly active users as of October 2025.
Key Technology: LLMs
Large Language Models (LLMs) are AI systems trained on enormous amounts of text to learn language patterns. They don't truly "understand" — they predict the most likely next word, sentence, or idea.

This is what powers ChatGPT, Gemini, and Copilot — the tools you'll be using in this seminar.
Source: Coursera Staff (2026). What Is ChatGPT?
Module 1 › Key Concepts at a Glance
Before We Move On
Five foundational concepts that will appear throughout this seminar.
Machine Learning
AI learns from examples rather than following hard-coded rules. Three types: supervised (labeled data), unsupervised (finding patterns), and reinforcement (trial-and-error with rewards).
Neural Networks & Deep Learning
Layers of artificial "neurons" inspired by the brain that can learn increasingly complex patterns. Deep learning stacks many layers to recognize faces, translate language, or generate images.
Large Language Models (LLMs)
Systems like GPT that learn statistical patterns in language to produce coherent text. They don't truly "understand" — they predict the most probable continuation based on training data.
Training Data
AI reflects whatever data it was trained on — including any biases in that data. The quality, diversity, and fairness of training data directly shapes AI behavior and outputs.
Prompt Engineering
Designing effective inputs (prompts) to guide AI outputs — a practical skill you'll develop in Module 6 of this seminar. The better your prompt, the better the AI's response.
Source: Coursera Staff (2025). How Does AI Work? Basics to Know.

Universities have deployed AI-enhanced LMS with personalized pathways. ChatGPT is used for second language writing (Barrot, 2024) and literature reviews in STEM disciplines. Implementation is largely experimental and course-specific.
🏫 K–12 Level
AI applications are more limited. A pilot of AI-driven personalized learning in science at select urban secondary schools showed improved engagement — but significant challenges in scaling. Elementary schools show minimal implementation (Fontanilla et al., 2023).
⚙️ Administrative Use
AI automates scheduling, document processing, and preliminary admission assessments across both K-12 and HEIs. Efficiency gains noted, but data privacy and security remain key concerns (Ramos et al., 2024).
🔬 Research Tools
Widespread adoption of ChatGPT for literature reviews and initial data analysis among library science and STEM students (Bula et al., 2025). Machine learning is increasingly incorporated into research methodologies.
Source: Co, S.J. (2025). AI in Philippine Education: A Narrative Review.
🇵🇭 Philippine Context › Teacher Perceptions
How Filipino Teachers View AI
Mixed attitudes — enthusiasm alongside real concerns
Strong confidence despite minimal preparation — 25 educators at Abueg National High School showed high confidence with little structured training (Sumandal, 2024).
Moderate awareness, positive attitudes — but concerns about overreliance on AI in instruction (Goli-Cruz, 2024).
Significant reservations about academic integrity and the fear of AI replacing instructional roles — especially among older faculty (Candilas et al., 2025; Suello et al., 2024).
Critical Gap (TPACK Framework): Technical knowledge is developing, but pedagogical integration remains a significant challenge among pre-service teachers (Bautista et al., 2024).
Source: Co, S.J. (2025). AI in Philippine Education: A Narrative Review.
🇵🇭 Philippine Context › Student Perceptions
How Filipino Students View AI
Generally more positive than faculty — but with nuance
Reduced cognitive load — Students using ChatGPT for academic support reported reduced stress and enhanced learning efficiency (Patac & Patac, 2025).
STEM vs. Humanities divide — Secondary students showed greater enthusiasm for AI in STEM; humanities students raised concerns about creativity and critical thinking (Suarez et al., 2025).
Awareness varies by program — Technology-focused majors showed greater AI awareness and positive perceptions (Villarosa, 2024).
Caution: Moderate AI integration yielded positive outcomes, but overreliance raises concerns about skill development (Bancoro, 2024).
Source: Co, S.J. (2025). AI in Philippine Education: A Narrative Review.
🇵🇭 Philippine Context › Challenge: Infrastructure
Challenge 1: Infrastructure & Digital Divide
🌐 Connectivity & Hardware
Significant infrastructure limitations in remote regions with restricted network access (Melchor et al., 2023). Rural HEIs face substantial challenges due to connectivity issues and outdated hardware (Villarino, 2025).
💰 Funding Constraints
High costs of AI acquisition, deployment, and maintenance are significant challenges for public institutions — resulting in piecemeal rather than systematic implementation (Digo et al., 2024).
🗺️ Urban-Rural Digital Divide
Urban schools in Zamboanga Peninsula demonstrate far greater AI adoption rates than rural counterparts (Balasa et al., 2025; Diaz, 2025). This pattern is consistent across multiple regions — a critical equity concern.
Source: Co, S.J. (2025). AI in Philippine Education: A Narrative Review.
🇵🇭 Philippine Context › Challenge: Knowledge & Policy
Challenge 2 & 3: Knowledge Gaps, Ethics & Policy
📚 Knowledge & Skills Gaps
Pre-service teachers' AI adoption restricted by perceived difficulty and lack of training (Alejandro et al., 2024). Teachers with specific ChatGPT training showed significantly greater integration and effectiveness (De Jesus, 2024).
⚖️ Academic Integrity & Data Privacy
Significant ethical concerns around plagiarism in AI-assisted academic writing (Gustilo et al., 2023). Students expressed data privacy concerns despite positive perceptions. Language barriers also impede use of primarily English-designed AI tools (Candilas, 2025).
📋 Policy & Governance Gaps
Clear absence of institutional AI regulations (Bongcac & Pabalan, 2024). Inconsistent approaches across institutions and lack of national guidance — creating uncertainty for educators seeking to implement AI responsibly (Piedad et al., 2025).
Cultural gap: Many AI systems were not designed with Filipino cultural contexts in mind, potentially limiting effectiveness (Calizo & Bauyot, 2025).
Source: Co, S.J. (2025). AI in Philippine Education: A Narrative Review.

Module 3 › AI Advantages
The Advantages of AI
Practical benefits — where AI clearly delivers value
⚡ Increased Efficiency
AI handles mundane, high-volume tasks — categorizing support tickets, extracting data from documents, composing simple responses — while humans focus on judgment-intensive work.
🕐 24/7 Availability
AI monitors systems continuously, responds to queries, and provides basic support outside working hours — filling gaps when humans are unavailable, not replacing them.
✅ Reduced Human Error
AI catches routine errors in data entry, record matching, and consistency checks — especially when humans are tired or working with inconsistent data. Humans handle exceptions and approvals.
📊 Better Decision-Making
AI scans large amounts of data and spots patterns humans might miss — especially when signals are spread across time or involve many variables. AI provides insights; humans make strategic decisions.

Module 3 › AI Disadvantages
The Disadvantages of AI
Real risks that require honest awareness
💼 Job Displacement
AI automates entry-level, mundane roles — jobs may not be eliminated but significantly reduced. Remaining employees are required to handle more complex tasks, risking burnout if the nature of work doesn't evolve.
💸 High Cost of Implementation
AI is not just a product to purchase. Hidden costs include: data cleansing, integration, security analysis, model assessment, continuous monitoring, change management, and maintenance.
⚖️ Bias in Algorithms
Bias in training data is replicated — or amplified — by AI. Overall accuracy may look high while specific groups experience discriminatory outcomes in hiring, lending, or access to services.
🔁 Over-Reliance on Technology
When teams over-rely on AI, critical thinking erodes, subject matter expertise fades, and small errors compound rapidly. Models also become outdated after system changes.

Module 4 › Three Modalities of Human-AI Interaction
Three Ways to Interact with AI
🤖
Automation
AI does work for you following explicit instructions. Best for repetitive, time-consuming, or data-intensive tasks.
🤝
Augmentation
You and AI collaboratively co-define and co-execute tasks. Best for enhancing creativity and complex thinking.
⚙️
Agency
You configure AI to work independently on future tasks — for yourself or others. Requires deep understanding of AI capabilities.
Source: Dakan & Feller (2025). Framework for AI Fluency v1.1.
Modality 1 — Automation
🤖 AI Performs the Task
Best For
Repetitive, time-consuming, or data-intensive tasks — anything with a clear, definable output where efficiency matters most.
Requirements
Clear task definition + quality control. You still review and verify the AI's output before using it.
Examples
Writing emails · Generating summaries · Creating basic quizzes · Drafting lesson objectives · Formatting documents
💬 Prompt Example — Automation
"Write a 5-item multiple-choice quiz about the causes of World War II for Grade 9 students. For each item, include: the question, four answer choices (A–D), the correct answer, and a one-sentence explanation."
💬 Prompt Example — Automation
"Generate a sample parent letter announcing a science fair next Friday. Include the event purpose, student instructions, judging criteria, and a reminder to wear school uniform. Tone: warm and professional."
Source: Dakan & Feller (2025). Framework for AI Fluency v1.1.
Modality 2 — Augmentation
🤝 AI Collaborates With You
Best For
Enhancing creativity — AI acts as a thinking partner. You bring vision and judgment; AI brings breadth, speed, and alternative perspectives.
How It Works
Dynamic back-and-forth dialogue. You share your work, AI responds, you refine, AI improves — iteration is the key to great results.
Examples for Teachers
Developing lesson plans together · Refining instructional materials · Co-writing differentiated activities · Improving rubrics
💬 Prompt Example — Augmentation
"I'm developing a lesson on the Philippine Revolution for Grade 8 Social Studies. Here's my draft outline: [paste outline]. What important events or perspectives am I missing? Suggest two inquiry-based activities suited for a Filipino public school classroom."
💬 Iteration Follow-Up Prompt
"Good, but the second activity you suggested requires internet access which my students don't have. Revise it into an offline, materials-free alternative. Keep the same learning objective."
Source: Dakan & Feller (2025). Framework for AI Fluency v1.1.
Modality 3 — Agency
⚙️ AI Acts Independently
What It Means
You configure AI to independently perform future tasks — for yourself or for others who will interact with the AI you set up. Think: building a tool, not just using one.
Requirements
Sophisticated understanding of AI capabilities and limitations. You are responsible for how the AI behaves with others.
Examples for Teachers
Building a study chatbot for students · Creating an AI quiz generator for your department · Setting up an automated feedback tool
💬 Prompt Example — Agency (System Prompt)
"You are an AI study assistant for Grade 8 students studying Philippine history. When a student asks a question, guide them using Socratic questioning — never give direct answers. Keep responses friendly, age-appropriate, and in simple English. If a student asks about something outside Philippine history, politely redirect them."
⚠️ Important Responsibility
In Agency modality, you are accountable for how the AI behaves with every student who uses it. This requires the highest level of diligence and testing.
Source: Dakan & Feller (2025). Framework for AI Fluency v1.1.

Module 4 › The 4 Ds › Delegation
D1 — Delegation
Should I use AI, and which tool?
Creative vision and strategic selection of the right AI tools and modalities to realize that vision.
🎯 Goal & Task Awareness
Envision effective goals. Analyze and deconstruct tasks into what AI can do, what humans should do, and what benefits from collaboration.
🔧 Platform Awareness
Know different AI platforms' strengths and limitations. Evaluate tools based on project requirements, budget, and any legal or regulatory considerations.
⚖️ Task Delegation
Optimally assign work between AI and human. Understand which modality — Automation, Augmentation, or Agency — fits the task at hand.
💭
Ask yourself first: Is this task actually suitable for AI? Would it produce better results than I could achieve without AI in the same time? What could go wrong?
Source: Dakan & Feller (2025). Framework for AI Fluency v1.1.
D2 — Description
How do I communicate my vision to AI?
The art and science of crafting prompts that produce useful AI outputs.
Product Description
Clearly articulate desired characteristics and qualities. Translate your vision into terms AI can act on.
Process Description
Guide iterative collaboration. Break complex tasks into smaller prompts. Engage in dynamic back-and-forth dialogue.
Performance Description
Specify how AI should behave or interact — anticipating user needs and translating them into behavioral guidelines.
💬 Weak Prompt vs. Strong Prompt
❌ Weak:
"Make me a lesson plan."
✅ Strong:
"Create a 60-minute lesson plan on the causes of World War I for Grade 9 students in a Philippine public school. Include: learning objectives (SMART format), a hook activity (5 mins), direct instruction (15 mins), a group activity students can do without internet access, and an assessment. Use MELC-aligned learning outcomes."
Key Principle: The more specific your prompt — context, format, audience, constraints — the more useful the AI's output will be.
Source: Dakan & Feller (2025). Framework for AI Fluency v1.1.
Module 4 › The 4 Ds › Discernment
D3 — Discernment
Is this output good enough?
Critical evaluation of AI-generated outputs — understanding quality, relevance, bias, and accuracy.
🔍 Product Discernment
Critically assess quality, relevance, and effectiveness of AI output. Identify strengths and weaknesses. Never assume accuracy — always verify facts, citations, and claims.
🔄 Process Discernment
Evaluate the collaboration itself — is the back-and-forth producing better results than either human or AI alone? Know when to adjust your approach or abandon AI for this task.
🎭 Performance Discernment
Assess whether AI configured for Agency is behaving as intended — across different users and scenarios. Gather feedback and refine the AI-driven experience continuously.
🚨 Critical Questions to Ask
  • → Is this factually accurate? Can I verify it?
  • → Is there hidden bias in this output?
  • → Is this appropriate for my students?
  • → Would I be comfortable putting my name on this?
Source: Dakan & Feller (2025). Framework for AI Fluency v1.1.
Module 4 › The 4 Ds › Diligence
D4 — Diligence
Am I using AI responsibly?
Taking responsibility and vouching for final products created using AI assistance.
🛡️ Creation Diligence
Apply ethical principles throughout the creative process. Actively identify and mitigate biases and ethical risks in AI-assisted content before it reaches students.
👁️ Transparency Diligence
Understand audience and legal expectations. Clearly communicate AI involvement in your work — to students, colleagues, and institutions.
✅ Deployment Diligence
Thoroughly fact-check and test AI outputs before deployment. Manage and assume full responsibility for potential risks, impacts, and consequences.
⚖️
The Bottom Line: Using AI does not transfer responsibility. If you submit, publish, or share AI-assisted work — you are responsible for every word in it.
Source: Dakan & Feller (2025). Framework for AI Fluency v1.1.
